# YouTube Script Writer

Write YouTube scripts that maximize retention, deliver value per minute, and stand out from competitors. Every script follows a battle-tested framework covering research, hooks, payoffs, open loops, calls to action, and editing.

## Process

### Step 1: Gather Context

Ask the user:
1. **What is the video topic?** Get the core subject, angle, or thesis.
2. **Who is the target audience?** (beginners, intermediate, experts, general)
3. **What niche/channel is this for?** Understanding the niche shapes tone, depth, and competitor landscape.
4. **What is the video format?** (tutorial, listicle, documentary, storytelling, review, deep dive, case study, quick tips)
5. **Desired video length?** (short 2-5 min, medium 6-12 min, long 13+ min) — or ask for guidance on ideal length.
6. **What is the goal?** (educate, entertain, convert to product/service, grow subscribers, build authority)
7. **Any specific points they want covered?** Let the user provide their unique insights, personal stories, or must-include topics.
8. **Is there a CTA?** (product, lead magnet, subscribe, comment prompt, etc.)

### Step 2: Research (if requested or needed)

If the user wants help with research, follow the **Research Framework** below. If they already have their research and points, skip to Step 3.

### Step 3: Structure the Script

Using the frameworks below, build the script in this order:
1. **Plot Promise** — define what promise the title/thumbnail makes before writing anything
2. **First 5 Seconds** — meet the plot promise visually and verbally
3. **Intro** (up to 45 seconds total) — context, curiosity gaps, and USP using the Intro Writing Framework
4. **Payoffs** (main body) — the core value sections, connected by open loops
5. **CTA** (mid-roll and/or end) — woven naturally into the script
6. **Closing** — final payoff or memorable takeaway

### Step 4: Write the Script

Write the full script as a document with these conventions:
- **Speaker directions** in brackets: `[cut to screen recording]`, `[show B-roll of...]`, `[pause for emphasis]`
- **Section headers** clearly marked for the creator to navigate
- **Estimated timestamps** for each major section
- Write in the creator's natural speaking voice — conversational, not robotic
- Every sentence must earn its place. No filler. No fluff. High value per minute.

### Step 5: Edit and Refine

Run the script through the **Script Editing Checklist** below. Fix any issues before presenting.

### Step 6: Present to User

Deliver the script with:
- A summary of the structure and estimated total length
- Section-by-section breakdown with timestamps
- Any notes on storyboarding / visual suggestions
- Ask for feedback and iterate

## Research Framework

Research has four parts. Cover each one before writing.

### 1. Audience Understanding

Know what the target audience needs, wants, and cares about.
- What problems are they trying to solve?
- What language do they use? (avoid jargon they won't know)
- What's their current knowledge level?
- What would make them click on this video AND watch to the end?

### 2. Competitor Analysis

Spot trends, gaps, and winning strategies in the niche.
- Search the topic on YouTube. Identify the top 3-5 performing videos.
- For each video, note:
  - View count and publish date (to gauge performance)
  - Video length
  - The 3-5 main points covered
  - Hook style and effectiveness
  - Comments: FAQs, complaints, "you should cover X" requests
  - Retention peaks (if available) — what moments do viewers care most about?
- Grade each video using the **Competitor Grading System** below.
- Identify content gaps: what are competitors NOT covering that the audience wants?

### 3. Gather Content

Collect facts, examples, and unique insights that add value.
- Source information from books, studies, expert interviews, personal experience
- Find unique angles that competitors haven't covered
- Collect specific examples, data points, and stories that support each point
- Prioritize actionable, practical information over theory

### 4. Validate

Double-check information for accuracy and relevance.
- Verify facts, statistics, and claims
- Ensure the information is current and not outdated
- Confirm that the points serve the target audience (not just what the creator finds interesting)

## Competitor Grading System

When analyzing competitor videos, grade each on a 1-3 scale across six categories:

| Category | What to Evaluate | Basic (1) | Competent (2) | Exceptional (3) |
|----------|-----------------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Insight** | Originality and actionability of information | Repeats common knowledge, lacks depth | Helpful tips but ideas aren't unique | Unique, actionable insights that surprise the audience |
| **Hooks** | Effectiveness of opening 15-30 seconds | Generic opening, doesn't stand out | Interesting but doesn't clearly show why viewer should care | Instantly grabs attention with emotional, shocking, or relatable statement |
| **Pacing** | Rhythm and flow — too slow, too fast, or just right | Slow, drawn out, disorganized | Mostly engaging with some slow/rushed sections | Consistently engaging, balanced between quick delivery and well-timed pauses |
| **Tonality** | How well tone matches content and audience | Monotone, mismatched with audience | Generally appropriate but lacks dynamic energy | Perfect match for topic/audience with dynamic energy and emotional connection |
| **Storytelling** | Clarity and structure of narrative | No clear narrative, disjointed | Decent structure but stories don't enhance understanding | Masterful storytelling with personal anecdotes that enhance relatability |
| **Editing** | Visuals, audio, transitions | Minimal editing, poor quality | Functional but unremarkable | Seamless transitions, creative use of effects to enhance engagement |

**How to use the grades:**
- Identify which categories score lowest across competitors — these are your opportunities to overindex
- Note specific improvement ideas for each low-scoring category
- Build these improvements into your script

## Video Length Guidelines

The wrong length loses viewers. Too short means missing details. Too long means viewers click away.

### Choosing the Right Length

| Length | Duration | Best For | Characteristics |
|--------|----------|----------|----------------|
| **Short** | 2-5 min | Quick tips, comparisons, skits | No detailed instructions, fast-paced, 1-2 main points |
| **Medium** | 6-12 min | Tutorials, reviews, how-tos | 3-5 main points, detailed but concise, most important info only |
| **Long** | 13+ min | Deep dives, case studies, courses | 5-6+ main points, detailed instructions, comprehensive coverage |

### Key Principles
- **Longer does NOT mean more fluff.** Every minute must deliver value. A 25-minute video must be 25 minutes of high-value content, not 10 minutes stretched with padding.
- **Value per minute is everything.** It should be impossible for the viewer to NOT want to watch from start to finish in one sitting.
- **Match the depth to the length.** Short videos = surface-level tips. Long videos = deep, comprehensive breakdowns. Don't mismatch.
- **Look at what's working in the niche.** If the top-performing videos on this topic are 12 minutes, that's a strong signal.

## Picking the Right Points (Payoffs)

The points you cover are equally important as the video length. Great videos engage the audience by delivering exactly what they came for.

### How to Choose Points

1. **Watch the top 3-5 competitor videos.** Write down each video's key points.
2. **Check comments on competitor videos.** Look for FAQs, complaints, and "you should cover X" requests.
3. **Identify content gaps.** What are competitors NOT talking about that the audience clearly wants?
4. **Build on existing content, don't copy it.** Create a thesis for the gap, then script your video to fill it.
5. **Ask: "What is lacking from other videos out there?"** Your script should answer this.

### Common Mistakes When Picking Points

- **Covering too many points.** In a 10-minute video, 8 points means no depth. Choose fewer points and go deeper.
- **Choosing points YOU like but your audience doesn't care about.** Audience first. What does the viewer find most valuable?
- **Repeating the same points everyone else uses.** Have your own unique insights. Use research to find what's missing.

### Quality Check for Each Point

Every point in the script must be:
- **Relevant** to what the viewer clicked for
- **Actionable** — the viewer can DO something with this information
- **Valuable** — it adds real insight, not just restates the obvious
- **Unique** — it offers a perspective competitors haven't covered (or covers it better)

## Intro Writing Framework

The intro is everything from second 0 to roughly second 45. It's where most creators lose up to 50% of their viewers. The intro has four steps, executed in order.

### Step 1: Define Your Plot Promise

Before writing a single word, define your **plot promise** — the promise of what the video is going to be about, set by the title and thumbnail.

Your title and thumbnail don't just make people click — they set expectations. The intro's job is to meet those expectations immediately.

**Critical rule:** Don't write the intro first and then get locked into a title/thumbnail that people won't click on. Instead:
1. Start with the most clickable version of your title and thumbnail
2. Use that to define your plot promise
3. Build out the intro to match that promise

**Examples:**
- Title: "I Flew 7,292 Miles to Eat Here" → Plot promise: a video about attempting to eat at a specific far-away place
- Title: "Why You Should Stop Posting Every Day" → Plot promise: a video arguing against daily posting with evidence

### Step 2: The First 5 Seconds

The very beginning of your video has one job: **meet the plot promise set by the title and thumbnail.**

If a viewer clicks expecting one thing and gets something else, they assume clickbait and leave. To minimize drop-off from the very start:

**Say the subject of your title AND show the subject of your thumbnail within the first 5 seconds.**

- **Verbally** fulfill the plot promise — say what the video is about
- **Visually** fulfill the plot promise — show what the thumbnail promised

The visual component is more important than ever because of **autoplay/hover preview**. More viewers are hovering over videos before clicking, using the silent preview to decide whether to watch. If you rely solely on words to fulfill the plot promise, the silent preview tells the viewer nothing.

**Test:** Watch your first 5 seconds on mute. Can a viewer still tell what the video is about? If not, add visual cues.

Your first 5 seconds don't need to match the title word-for-word or the thumbnail pixel-for-pixel — just close enough to assure viewers they'll get what they clicked for.

### Step 3: The Rest of the Intro (aim for under 45 seconds total)

After the first 5 seconds, the rest of the intro must contain three core ingredients. They can appear in any order, but all three need to be there — and they need to be there fast.

#### Ingredient 1: Minimum Necessary Context

Context sets up the "what" and the "why" — bringing the viewer up to speed so they can appreciate the rest of the video.

**The danger:** Too much context upfront bloats **time to value** — the time between a viewer clicking and receiving the first piece of value they came for. The longer the time to value, the more impatient the viewer becomes.

**Rules:**
- Establish just enough context to get the viewer to care
- Fill in additional information as needed later in the video
- Ask: "What's the minimum amount of context I can provide to get the viewer on board?"
- Be ruthless with edits — if context can be moved to later, move it

**Example (Mark Rober):** "This is me on top of a speeding train, and this is me in a hot air balloon, to answer some of the most debated science questions on the internet." — One sentence. That's all the context needed.

#### Ingredient 2: Curiosity Gaps

A curiosity gap is the gap between what we currently know and what we want to know. Our brains are biologically wired to crave closure — when presented with an unanswered question, it creates a mental itch that can only be scratched by watching.

**Three techniques for opening curiosity gaps:**

| Technique | How It Works | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Contradiction** | Present two pieces of information that seem to conflict. The disconnect triggers "wait, what?" | "The pilot says he can land anywhere he wants. But there's nothing here that resembles a steering wheel." |
| **Verbalization** | Ask the question out loud yourself. Planting the question explicitly puts it in the viewer's mind. | "Which begs the question — how do you actually steer these things?" |
| **Interruption** | Start to answer, then cut off. The viewer knows the answer is coming but doesn't have it yet. | Show the pilot beginning to answer, then cut away before the explanation. |

**Stack multiple curiosity gaps.** The best intros open 2-3 curiosity gaps in under 45 seconds — each one is another reason the viewer can't leave.

**You can also create curiosity through simple promises.** Merely promising that there are X more things ahead inherently prompts the viewer to wonder what they are.

**Important:** If you're struggling to come up with strong curiosity gaps, the core video idea might not be interesting enough. Good curiosity gaps are born from conflict, tension, and transformation. If the concept doesn't set the stage for those elements, there's nothing to hook the viewer with.

#### Ingredient 3: Unique Service Proposition (USP)

Your USP is the reason someone would watch YOUR video instead of all the others on the same topic. In a world of infinite content, viewers aren't just deciding whether to watch — they're deciding which version of this topic is most worth watching.

**Four ways to establish USP:**

**1. Expertise** — Signal domain knowledge or authority.
- Explicit: "I'm Professor Ruth Bengat. I study authoritarianism." (one sentence, credibility established)
- Implicit: Wearing scrubs (Dr. Mike), having a professional setup, or demonstrating deep knowledge through delivery style
- Don't list your resume — signal expertise through context, not credentials

**2. Effort** — Show the work that went into the video. We naturally value things more when we know they took significant time, money, or energy (input bias).
- "I'm about to spend $10,000 in the next 4 minutes buying the craziest transparent tech on the internet"
- Mentioning how many books you read, showing time-lapses of prep work, or editing in a way that clearly takes skill
- You don't need to spend big money — just show that you did something most people wouldn't or couldn't

**3. Personality** — Your specific flavor, tone, and energy that makes your video irreplaceable.
- Your personality isn't just "be yourself" — it's the specific lens your video filters through
- Can be goofy, calm, skeptical, warm, chaotic — whatever is authentically yours
- When personality is upfront and clear, the video becomes an experience viewers can't get elsewhere

**4. Proof** — Show that you actually did the thing you promised. In a world of bold claims without payoff, showing the result upfront builds trust.
- This is "Jenga storytelling" — everyone knows the tower falls, but the fun is watching HOW
- Showing the result early doesn't spoil the story — it proves there's a story worth sticking around for
- Creates anticipation: the viewer has seen the destination and now wants to see the journey

**Stack multiple USP types for maximum effect.** The best intros combine 2-3: proof (showing the result) + effort (stunning visuals, multiple locations) + personality (authentic reactions).

### Step 4: Front-Load the Viewing Experience

Your intro serves as a proxy for the entire video — viewers judge the rest based on what they see in the intro. If your best visuals, animations, or production quality only show up later, viewers may never know they exist.

**Rules:**
- If you use multiple types of footage throughout the video, show that visual variety in the intro
- If you have a custom animation later, make a shorter version for the intro
- Annotate your script line by line: what does the viewer HEAR vs. what do they SEE?
- Your intro should reflect the quality of the full video — lead with your best

## Hook Types

Use these proven hook structures for the first line of your intro. Pick the one that best fits the video topic and niche.

### Elements of an Effective Hook

- **Clear.** The viewer instantly understands what this video is about. No confusion.
- **No jargon.** Keep the hook accessible to the broadest possible audience, then niche down later.
- **Engage the viewer directly.** Address "you" — talk TO the viewer, not AT the world.

#### 1. The Bold Claim
Open with a surprising or counterintuitive statement that challenges assumptions.
> "You don't need 10,000 hours to master a skill. In fact, that whole idea is based on a misunderstanding."

#### 2. The Story Hook
Drop the viewer into the middle of a compelling story. Create curiosity about what happened.
> "Last year, I was $50,000 in debt with no job and no plan. Twelve months later, I'd built a six-figure business."

#### 3. The Question Hook
Ask a question that the viewer desperately wants answered.
> "What if everything you've been told about productivity is actually making you less productive?"

#### 4. The Contrarian Hook
Challenge a widely accepted belief head-on.
> "Everyone says you need to wake up at 5 AM to be successful. That's complete nonsense, and here's the data to prove it."

#### 5. The "Have You Ever" Hook
Tap into a universal experience that creates instant relatability.
> "Have you ever studied for hours and still felt like nothing stuck? There's a scientific reason for that."

#### 6. The Result Hook
Lead with the impressive outcome, then promise to show how.
> "This simple framework helped me go from 0 to 100,000 subscribers in 8 months. And today, I'm going to break down exactly how it works."

#### 7. The Stakes Hook
Make the viewer feel the cost of NOT watching.
> "If you're making this one mistake with your money, you could be losing thousands of dollars a year without even realizing it."

#### 8. The Pattern Interrupt
Say something unexpected that forces a double-take.
> "Stop. Before you write another script, I need you to delete the first paragraph."

#### 9. The "Easy Actually" Hook
Take something the audience thinks is hard and reframe it as achievable.
> "Becoming less addicted to your phone is easy, actually. And I can prove it in 10 minutes."

#### 10. The Credibility + Promise Hook
Briefly establish why you're worth listening to, then promise a specific outcome.
> "After writing scripts for channels with over 50 million combined views, I've found that every viral video follows one pattern. And I'm about to show it to you."

### Hook Mistakes to Avoid

- **Overexplaining.** Don't sell the viewer too hard on why they should watch. Just hook them.
- **Excessive credentials.** Don't spend the first 30 seconds listing your resume. Weave credibility into the content.
- **Being vague.** "This video will change your life" tells the viewer nothing. Be specific.
- **Using jargon.** The hook is the widest part of the funnel. Keep it accessible.
- **Starting with "Hey guys, welcome back to my channel."** Wasted seconds. Get to the hook immediately.

## Open Loops

Open loops are transitions between sections that keep the viewer watching. They create curiosity about what's coming next BEFORE closing the current section.

### How Open Loops Work

An open loop plants a question or teases upcoming value so the viewer can't click away without getting the answer.

### Structure

1. **Close the current payoff.** Deliver the value you promised for this section.
2. **Tease the next section.** Before moving on, hint at why the next section is even more important.
3. **Create a gap.** The viewer should feel "I need to hear this next part."

### Examples

> "So now you know how to find the right points for your video. But here's the thing — even the best points will fall flat if you don't know how to structure them. And the structure I'm about to show you is responsible for some of the highest-retention videos on YouTube."

> "That's the first mistake. But the second mistake is actually worse, and almost nobody talks about it."

> "Okay, so that's how you write the hook. But the hook is useless if the next 60 seconds don't deliver. Let me show you exactly what comes after."

### Rules for Open Loops

- **Don't overuse them.** One per transition is enough. Using them every 30 seconds feels manipulative.
- **Deliver on every open loop.** If you promise something, you MUST pay it off. Broken loops kill trust.
- **Make them natural.** They should feel like a logical bridge, not a gimmick.
- **Vary the style.** Don't always use the same formula. Mix teases, questions, and stakes.

## Call to Action (CTA) Framework

CTAs convert viewers into subscribers, customers, or community members. Place them strategically — not just at the end.

### Mid-Roll CTA (during the video)

Place a CTA after you've delivered significant value — typically after the 2nd or 3rd payoff, when the viewer trusts you and is engaged.

**Rules:**
- **Earn the CTA.** Only ask after you've given real value. The viewer should feel like "this person really knows their stuff."
- **Keep it brief.** 10-15 seconds max. Don't break the flow.
- **Make it relevant.** Connect the CTA to what you just taught. "If you want the template I just walked through, link in the description."
- **Use soft CTAs for subscribe:** "If this is helpful so far, consider subscribing — we go deep on [niche] every week."

### End CTA (closing of the video)

The final CTA should feel like a natural conclusion, not a sales pitch tacked on.

**Rules:**
- **Summarize the value first.** Remind them what they just learned.
- **Connect to the next step.** "Now that you know X, the next thing you need is Y — and I cover that in this video."
- **Be specific about what to do.** Don't just say "check the description." Say what's there and why it matters.
- **End strong.** The last sentence should be memorable and motivating, not "don't forget to like and subscribe."

## Script Structure Template

Use this as the skeleton for every script. Adjust section count and depth based on video length.

```
=== INTRO (0:00 - 0:45) ===
[Plot promise: what does the title/thumbnail promise?]

-- First 5 Seconds --
[SAY the subject of the title]
[SHOW the subject of the thumbnail]
[Hook type: bold claim / story / question / etc.]

-- Rest of Intro (up to 0:45) --
[Minimum necessary context — just enough to get the viewer to care]
[Curiosity gaps — 2-3 unanswered questions using contradiction, verbalization, or interruption]
[USP — why watch YOUR video? expertise / effort / personality / proof]
[Front-load visual quality — reflect the production value of the full video]
[Open loop into first payoff]

=== PAYOFF 1 (1:30 - X:XX) ===
[Section header]
[Core teaching / insight / story]
[Example or proof]
[Open loop into next section]

=== PAYOFF 2 (X:XX - X:XX) ===
[Section header]
[Core teaching / insight / story]
[Example or proof]
[Open loop into next section]

=== MID-ROLL CTA (after payoff 2 or 3) ===
[Brief, relevant CTA — subscribe / product / lead magnet]

=== PAYOFF 3 (X:XX - X:XX) ===
[Continue with remaining payoffs...]
[Each connected by open loops]

=== CLOSING (last 30-60 seconds) ===
[Summarize key takeaways]
[Final CTA]
[Memorable closing line]
```

### Payoff Count by Video Length

| Video Length | Number of Payoffs | Depth per Payoff |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Short (2-5 min) | 1-2 | Surface-level, quick tips |
| Medium (6-12 min) | 3-5 | Detailed, with examples |
| Long (13+ min) | 5-8 | Deep, with examples, stories, and actionable steps |

## Script Editing Checklist

After writing the first draft, run through this checklist. Fix every issue before presenting.

### Intro Check
- [ ] Is the plot promise clearly defined before writing?
- [ ] Do the first 5 seconds SAY the subject of the title and SHOW the subject of the thumbnail?
- [ ] Would a viewer watching the first 5 seconds on mute still understand what the video is about?
- [ ] Is the total intro 45 seconds or less?
- [ ] Is context minimal — just enough to get the viewer to care, with the rest filled in later?
- [ ] Are there at least 2 curiosity gaps opened (using contradiction, verbalization, or interruption)?
- [ ] Is the USP established (expertise, effort, personality, or proof)?
- [ ] Does the intro front-load the visual quality of the full video?
- [ ] Is it free of jargon and accessible to the broadest audience?
- [ ] Is there NO "hey guys welcome back" or throat-clearing?

### Value Check
- [ ] Does every section deliver actionable, specific value?
- [ ] Is there any filler or fluff that can be cut?
- [ ] Would the viewer feel they got MORE than they expected?
- [ ] Are the points unique — not just a copy of what competitors say?
- [ ] Is every sentence earning its place in the script?

### Flow Check
- [ ] Do open loops connect each section naturally?
- [ ] Does the pacing feel right — not too rushed, not dragging?
- [ ] Are transitions smooth between sections?
- [ ] Is every open loop paid off later in the script?

### Audience Check
- [ ] Is the language appropriate for the target audience's knowledge level?
- [ ] Does the script address the viewer directly ("you")?
- [ ] Would the target audience watch this to the end?
- [ ] Does it answer the question the viewer had when they clicked?

### CTA Check
- [ ] Is the mid-roll CTA placed after delivering real value?
- [ ] Is the CTA brief and relevant (not a jarring interruption)?
- [ ] Does the end CTA feel natural and motivating?
- [ ] Is there a clear next step for the viewer?

### Speakability Check
- [ ] Read the script out loud. Does it sound natural and conversational?
- [ ] Are sentences short enough to deliver in one breath?
- [ ] Are there natural pause points for emphasis?
- [ ] Would the creator actually say these words, or do they sound "written"?

## Storyboarding Notes

After the script is written, add visual direction notes to help the creator or editor plan the visuals.

For each section, suggest:
- **On-screen text** or lower thirds for key points
- **B-roll ideas** that reinforce the point being made
- **Screen recordings** if demonstrating a tool or process
- **Graphics or diagrams** for complex concepts
- **Cut points** where the edit should change pace or angle

Format these as inline brackets in the script:
```
[B-ROLL: time-lapse of someone working at a desk]
[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Step 1: Research your competitors"]
[CUT TO: screen recording of YouTube search]
[GRAPHIC: show the 4-part research framework]
```

## Tone and Voice Guidelines

- **Conversational, not academic.** Write like the creator talks to a friend, not like a textbook.
- **Direct and confident.** No hedging. Say "do this" not "you might want to consider maybe doing this."
- **Energetic but not manic.** Match the energy to the niche. Business content is confident and calm. Entertainment content is higher energy.
- **Use "you" constantly.** The viewer should feel like you're talking directly to them.
- **Short sentences for impact.** Long sentences for flow. Mix them.
- **Avoid filler phrases:** "without further ado," "let's dive in," "so yeah," "that being said."
- **Match the creator's natural voice.** If you have examples of their previous content, mirror their speaking style, cadence, and vocabulary.

## AI-Assisted Workflow

When using AI (like this skill) to assist with script writing, follow this workflow:

1. **Research first, AI second.** Do the competitor analysis and audience research before asking AI to write. Feed AI the research findings, not just the topic.
2. **Provide context.** Give AI the niche, audience, competitor gaps, unique insights, and personal stories. The more context, the better the output.
3. **Iterate in sections.** Don't try to generate the entire script in one shot. Work through hook, then payoffs, then transitions.
4. **Add the human layer.** AI provides structure and initial draft. The creator adds personal stories, unique insights, and their authentic voice.
5. **Edit ruthlessly.** Cut anything that sounds generic, robotic, or like it could appear on any channel. The script should only work for THIS creator on THIS channel.
