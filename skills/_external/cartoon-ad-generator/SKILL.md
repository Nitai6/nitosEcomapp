# Cartoon Ad Generator

Original illustrated ad creatives — not meme templates, not stock photos. These are custom visual scenes that tell a story in a single image. A knight fighting a hydra of tasks. Two doors: one labeled "the old way," one glowing with your product. A race track where everyone else is in race cars and your audience is on a bicycle.

The visual concept does the selling. Text supports it — it doesn't carry it.

---

## Ad Psychology Framework

### The 6 Psychological Triggers

1. **Transformation** — Show the before/after. The viewer sees themselves in "before" and desires "after." Most powerful when the gap is dramatic and specific.
2. **Social Proof** — Numbers, momentum, crowd following. Makes the choice feel safe and obvious.
3. **Curiosity Gap** — A visual tension that demands resolution. "What happens next?" forces the click.
4. **FOMO/Scarcity** — The train is leaving. Show real momentum — the gap is widening.
5. **Authority/Education** — Teach something valuable through the image. The insight is the hook.
6. **Emotional Resonance** — Pure feeling. Confidence, freedom, relief, belonging. The product becomes a symbol, not a thing.

### Visual Psychology Rules

- **Contrast = attention.** The element with highest contrast gets seen first. Place it deliberately.
- **Faces attract.** Characters with visible emotion are attention magnets — even illustrated ones.
- **Scale tells the story.** One person vs. an army. One bicycle vs. ten race cars. Scale contrast IS the message.
- **Color carries emotion:**
  - Red/dark → danger, wrong choice, old way
  - Green/gold/light → right choice, growth, the product's world
  - Dark backgrounds → premium, focused, scroll-stopping (light backgrounds disappear in feeds)
- **Negative space = premium.** Don't fill every corner. Let the concept breathe.

### Composition Framework

Divide the canvas into three zones:
- **Top 30% (Hook Zone):** Headline or scene-setting element. The viewer reads this first.
- **Middle 40% (Focal Zone):** The primary visual concept. This does the persuasion work.
- **Bottom 30% (Conversion Zone):** Supporting context, social proof number, or CTA.

**Eye-flow patterns:**
- **Z-pattern:** Top-left hook → top-right → diagonal down → bottom-right CTA
- **Vertical:** Top-to-bottom for mobile. Focal point in upper 60%, context below.

### Text on Creative Rules

- **Headline:** 2-6 words, bold, readable at mobile size. Communicates a specific benefit or outcome.
- **Supporting line:** 1 short phrase adding context (price, timeframe, stat, CTA)
- All-caps for short emphasis only — never for full sentences
- Dark gradient fade behind text areas so visuals can be bigger while text stays readable
- 2 typefaces maximum per ad
- **The 2-second test:** Can someone understand what's being offered, why it's valuable, and what to do next — from the image alone?

### Visual Style Defaults

**Dark-first palette:**
| Element | Color | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Background | Near-black (#0A0A0A) | Flat, no gradients on the bg itself |
| Primary text | White (#FFFFFF) | Headlines, labels |
| Accent | Gold (#C9A84C) | CTAs, price, "right choice" highlights |
| Wrong-choice indicator | Red (#FF4444) | The old way, the bad door, the enemy |
| Right-choice indicator | Electric blue or gold | The product's world |

**Why dark backgrounds?** They pop against Meta's white/blue chrome. Light ads disappear in feeds; dark ads stop the scroll.

## Proven Illustrated Scene Concepts

These visual concepts work across niches because they tap universal human experiences:

### Two Doors
A person standing between two doors — one ominous/red (the old way or the wrong choice), one glowing/gold or green (your product). Classic visual metaphor for a binary decision.
- **Marketing angle:** Comparison, Pain Point
- **Headline:** "CHOOSE WISELY" or "[Product] or keep doing it the hard way"
- **Key:** The glow on the right door draws the eye. Make the wrong door feel genuinely bad (cracked, dark, red light underneath).
- **Works for:** Any product that replaces a painful status quo

### Solo Fighter vs. Army
One person fighting alone vs. commanding an army of helpers. The scale contrast IS the message.
- **Marketing angle:** Pain Point → Aspirational, FOMO
- **Headline:** "STOP FIGHTING ALONE" or "You vs. you with [Product]"
- **Key:** The gap between the sides should be dramatic — not subtle. 1 tired person vs. 20 soldiers/robots.
- **Works for:** Tools, services, communities, anything that gives leverage

### Isometric Cutaway (Two Rooms)
3D isometric cross-section showing two contrasting rooms side by side: one chaotic/dark (without your product), one organized/glowing (with your product). Like a dollhouse cross-section.
- **Marketing angle:** Comparison, Aspirational
- **Example:** Left room = person buried in manual work at 2am. Right room = same person sleeping while automated systems work.
- **Key:** Both sides visible simultaneously — the contrast is undeniable.
- **Works for:** Productivity tools, automation, services that do the work for you

### Race Track
A race scene showing the audience falling behind while others speed ahead. Makes FOMO visual and specific.
- **Marketing angle:** FOMO, Comparison
- **Headline:** "THE RACE IS NOT EVEN CLOSE"
- **Key:** The gap between vehicles should be extreme. Bicycle vs. race cars vs. rocket ship.
- **Works for:** Any market where speed/leverage matters

### Before/After Diagonal Split
Image split diagonally — left/top is "without" (stressed, gray, overwhelmed), right/bottom is "with" (relaxed, colorful, winning). More dynamic than a flat 50/50 split.
- **Marketing angle:** Transformation, Pain Point + Aspirational
- **Headline:** "[Timeframe] from now" or "Before / After"
- **Key:** The states should be dramatically different in color temperature (cold blue/gray before, warm gold/green after).
- **Works for:** Any product with a clear before/after transformation

### Control Room / Command Center
The "after" state: a person confidently monitoring dashboards and systems that run automatically. Power and control without stress.
- **Marketing angle:** Aspirational
- **Headline:** "THIS IS MY MONDAY MORNING" or "[Product] running while you sleep"
- **Key:** The screens show real metrics/activity. The person looks relaxed — not frantic. This is the dream state.
- **Works for:** Tools, automation, SaaS, anything that creates leverage

### Character Evolution (Left-to-Right Progression)
Show a character progressing through stages from left to right. Each stage more capable/confident. The rightmost stage is what your product makes possible.
- **Marketing angle:** Transformation, Aspirational
- **Example:** Stage 1: overwhelmed, hunched. Stage 2: getting organized. Stage 3: confident, powerful.
- **Headline:** "30 DAYS WITH [Product]" or "THE EVOLUTION"
- **Key:** The stages should look dramatically different — not subtle improvements.
- **Works for:** Education, communities, coaching, any product with a learning curve

### Comic Book Origin (3 Panels)
Three-panel horizontal comic strip: Panel 1 = the "before" struggle, Panel 2 = the discovery/turning point, Panel 3 = the "after" transformation. Classic superhero origin structure.
- **Marketing angle:** Transformation, Curiosity Gap
- **Style:** Bold graphic novel or comic book art style. High contrast, bold outlines, speed lines.
- **Key:** Each panel gets a short caption. The transformation should feel earned by Panel 3.
- **Works for:** Any product with a clear origin story or transformation journey

### Heist Crew / Team Lineup
Characters lined up like a movie poster — each labeled with their role. Shows what "assembling your team" looks like.
- **Marketing angle:** Direct Offer, Aspirational, Value Stack
- **Example:** "YOUR [PRODUCT] TEAM: [Role 1] • [Role 2] • [Role 3] • [Role 4]" lined up like a heist crew
- **Headline:** "[N] tools/agents/experts. ONE price."
- **Works for:** Communities, bundles, tools with multiple components, services with multiple deliverables

### Classroom / Whiteboard
A character teaching at a whiteboard or presenting. The whiteboard content IS the value prop (framework, steps, system).
- **Marketing angle:** Authority, Education
- **Key:** The whiteboard shows a real, clear framework (not abstract). The teacher character looks confident and knowledgeable.
- **Works for:** Courses, coaching, consulting, education products

### Expectation vs Reality (Subverted)
Two panels: "What you think you'll get" vs "What you actually get" — but subvert the format. Instead of showing the letdown, BOTH panels are great. The twist IS the hook.
- **Marketing angle:** Social Proof, Humor
- **Key:** The subversion needs to be obvious. Make Panel 1 look genuinely impressive, then Panel 2 tops it.
- **Works for:** Premium products that over-deliver, communities, bundles

## Process

### Step 1: Gather Product Info

Ask for the following if not provided:

- **Product name:** What are we advertising?
- **Core benefit:** What does it do for the customer? (one sentence)
- **Target audience:** Who is this for? (be specific)
- **Pain point:** What problem does this solve? What do they struggle with before finding this?
- **The transformation:** What does their work/life look like AFTER using this?
- **Price point:** (optional — helps calibrate messaging)
- **Tone:** Serious and premium? Playful and punchy? Confident and bold?
- **Brand assets available?** Logo PNG, mascot, screenshot? (optional — illustrated scenes can be fully original)

### Step 2: Generate 3 Concept Briefs

Pick 3 scene concepts that each take a **fundamentally different psychological approach**. Each must pass the Specificity Test: can you explain in one sentence what the ad is offering and why someone would click?

**Angles that will NOT pass:**
- Vague lifestyle: "Feel the freedom" with no hook
- Pure aesthetics: Cool illustration with no marketing message
- Generic motivation: "Unlock your potential"

**Angles that WILL pass:**
- Specific transformation with timeframe: "30 days from now, here's what changes"
- Clear comparison with named outcome: "Manual work vs. [Product] — here's the gap"
- Bold claim with visual proof: "[N] things running automatically while you sleep"

For each concept, define:

```
## Concept {1/2/3}: {Scene Name}

**Scene type:** {which proven concept above, or a new original concept}
**Psychological trigger:** {which of the 6 triggers}
**Marketing angle:** {Comparison / Pain Point / Aspirational / etc.}
**The visual story:** {2-3 sentences describing exactly what the image shows — characters, setting, action}
**Headline text (on creative):** "{exact headline}"
**Supporting text:** "{supporting line — price, stat, CTA, or timeframe}"
**Color approach:** {what's dark, what's lit up, what color signals the "right" choice}
**Why someone clicks:** {1 sentence — the specific reason a real person stops scrolling}
**Specificity check:** {what concrete number, timeframe, or outcome is promised?}
```

### Step 3: Gather Assets (if needed)

Illustrated scenes are often fully original — no reference images required. But for specific elements:

- **Logo or wordmark:** Pass as `--reference` if it should appear in the ad
- **Product screenshot:** If showing a UI element or dashboard, download/use a real screenshot
- **Character-specific elements:** If the user has a brand mascot or character, use it as a reference

For pre-generating elements (e.g., a custom character in a specific scene):
```bash
# Pre-generate a themed element first
python3 scripts/generate_ad.py \
  --prompt "Illustrated cartoon character in [themed scene]..." \
  --aspect-ratio "1:1" \
  --output "workspace/{today}/ads/{product-slug}/refs/custom-element.png"

# Then use it as a reference in the final ad
python3 scripts/generate_ad.py \
  --reference "workspace/{today}/ads/{product-slug}/refs/custom-element.png" \
  --prompt "{full ad prompt}" \
  --aspect-ratio "1:1" \
  --output "workspace/{today}/ads/{product-slug}/cartoon/v{N}-{scene-name}.png"
```

### Step 4: Generate All 3 Ads

Run all 3 generations concurrently.

```bash
python3 scripts/generate_ad.py \
  --prompt "{detailed scene prompt}" \
  --aspect-ratio "1:1" \
  --output "workspace/{today}/ads/{product-slug}/cartoon/v{N}-{scene-name}.png"
```

**Prompt construction for illustrated scenes:**

```
Illustrated cartoon ad creative for [PRODUCT].

Scene: [DETAILED SCENE DESCRIPTION — characters, setting, action, what's happening in each part of the image]

Layout:
- Top: Bold white headline "[HEADLINE]" in ALL CAPS
- Center: [MAIN SCENE DESCRIPTION]
- Bottom: Supporting text "[SUPPORTING LINE]" in smaller text

Style: [bold graphic novel / flat cartoon / comic book / painterly illustration] — dark near-black background (#0A0A0A), high contrast, vivid accents.

Colors: Dark background, white headline, [gold/green/electric blue] for the "right choice" elements, [red/dark] for the "wrong way" elements if applicable.

Gradient: Dark gradient overlay behind all text areas (top and bottom) so text stays readable while the center visual is full-bleed.

Do NOT add any logos or watermarks unless specified.
Do NOT add any extra text beyond what is specified.
Do NOT make it look corporate or stock-photo-like.
Do NOT use a light or white background.
```

**Tips for getting great illustrated scenes:**
- Describe the scene with camera-like precision: "A single exhausted person sits at a cluttered desk in the dark. To the right, the same person (relaxed, smiling) watches 20 glowing robotic workers at computer stations."
- Specify the art style: "bold flat cartoon," "comic book with ink outlines," "cinematic illustrated scene," "painterly with warm lighting"
- Repeat critical constraints 2x in the prompt
- Name colors explicitly: "near-black background (#0A0A0A)", "gold highlight (#C9A84C)"
- Anti-instructions: "Do NOT make it photorealistic", "Do NOT add any extra characters"

### Step 5: Review

**Click test:**
- [ ] Would someone actually stop scrolling for this?
- [ ] Is the offer/hook clear within 2 seconds?
- [ ] Does the visual tell the story without needing to read the text?
- [ ] Is there at least one specific number, timeframe, or outcome visible?

**Quality gates:**
- [ ] Text is readable and correctly spelled
- [ ] Dark background maintained (not light/white)
- [ ] Headline is prominent and readable at mobile size
- [ ] The visual concept matches what was specified
- [ ] No extra unintended text or elements were added

**Auto-reject and regenerate:**
- Light/white background used instead of dark
- Headline text has misspellings or is garbled
- Visual concept missed the brief entirely
- Looks like stock art or a motivational poster
- The marketing message isn't visible — it's just a pretty illustration
- Extra text appeared that wasn't specified

**Text rendering tips for regeneration:**
- Shorter headline = fewer typos. Cut to the minimum.
- Spell out critical words: "The word AUTOMATION, spelled A-U-T-O-M-A-T-I-O-N"
- Repeat headline text 2x in the prompt
- If a word keeps garbling, find a simpler synonym

### Step 6: Present

Show all 3 ads with:
- The ad image
- The scene concept and psychological angle
- Review status (pass/fail, any issues)
- Any text that needs post-production fixing

Ask which direction resonates and offer to iterate.

## Key Principles

1. **The visual does the selling.** Text supports — it doesn't carry the weight. If you have to explain the image, the image is wrong.
2. **Dark backgrounds always.** They pop against feed chrome. Light ads disappear.
3. **Scale contrast = story.** 1 vs. 20, before vs. after, alone vs. team — dramatic contrast communicates faster than any headline.
4. **Specificity test.** Every ad needs at least one concrete detail (a number, a timeframe, a specific outcome). Vague = skip.
5. **Pre-generate complex elements.** If an ad needs a custom character or specific scene element, generate it first and use it as a reference. Better than trying to describe everything in one prompt.

---

## What NOT to Do

- Cinematic stock-photo-style backgrounds (they look like motivational posters)
- White or light backgrounds
- Vague transformation without specifics ("change your life", "unlock your potential")
- Overcrowded composition with text competing against visuals
- Corporate color palettes (light blue, white, corporate gray)
- Generic office stock art with suits pointing at screens
- Overly polished "luxury" aesthetic — gold borders, marble, exclusive vibes (feels hollow)
